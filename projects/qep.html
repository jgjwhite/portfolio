User Research & Testing

Queen Elizabeth Park Community Centre

Design Challenge

As part of a user centered design course, a group of staff members from the Queen Elizabeth Park Community Centre visited our class and briefed us on their problem. The challenge put forward had to do with redesigning their online scheduling system to make it more intuitive and suitable for their audience.

We worked in teams throughout the semester to tackle this problem. Our roles were fluid and each of us contributed to the different process steps.

Problem

The staff leaders from Queen Elizabeth Park Centre reported that members expressed frustration with the online studio schedule. The website is complicated and difficult to use, so members resorted to dropping in. Since studios would often fill up, this lead to members having to turn around and head back home.

Objectives

Intuitive use
our audience should be able to easily navigate through the interface.

Enhance overall studio experience
the entire studio experience (before, during and after) should be taken into account during the design process.

Inclusive design
the interface should be friendly to a reasonable degree of impairments and accessibility challenges.

Site Vist & Stakeholder Interviews

We visited QEP and noticed that facility navigation was a challenge: there were no obvious site maps near the entrance. We found out that members rely on handwritten calendars posted on the studio doors, and when drop-in studios filled up during a given time, any newcomers would be turned away.

Journey Map

Journey mapping helped us explore our earlier observations in more depth. The online schedule was difficult to find on the QEP home page, and it was overly complex. Visitors would be turned away from studios if they were full, having to either choose a different studio or return at another time. There was also a lack of high visibility on-site facility navigation. Using these pain points, we saw opportunities to explore during our design phase.

User Scenarios

A scenario analysis pointed to a need to incorporate program registration (distinct from drop-in studios). Programs are structured courses offered to QEP members for learning different creative practices. At this stage, we began to tinker with the idea of an on-site kiosk; as a means to increase the visibility of both the map and schedule.

Testable Prototypes

Two prototypes were tested: a kiosk design (depicted above), which I focused on with one other group member, as well as a separate browser-only design. The browser design provided a comparison for participants to give feedback on.

User Testing

I drafted our testing brief, scenario and script for the on-site user test. Durign the test, we noted that there was an interest in the kiosk companion. Program registration had positive feedback, but the information architecture needed technical adjustments. Some examples of feedback that we received included: prioritizing the need for a safety certification before people could view or book certain studios, and participants also wanted an opt-out button when asked for personal information.

View Studio Availability

Users could now walk up to a kiosk, or visit the website, and immediately see the studio availability because we chose to move the schedule to the home screen. The visual design was based on familiarity, which was considered an important factor for improving the ease of use. Accessibility considerations were taking into account; colour choices were tested using a WCAG contrast checker, and type size was increased across the interface.

Program Registration

An opt-out toggle was added to any personal or financial information requests (beyond e-mail addresses). The user could now elect to pay at the front desk in the revised prototype, and the labelling system was adjusted based on our testing. For example; “classes” was changed to “programs,” with specific “courses” to register with.

View Facility Map

The added tab bar enabled a link to the QEP map to be available on every screen. Visitors could tap the icon at the kiosk, and view a map of QEP from any screen within the interface. We also added a map search feature, where visitors could quickly focus the map on one of the creative studios.

Reflection

Presenting our prototype to QEP staff let us re-examine our design objectives and weigh them against staff feedback.

Ease of Use.
Visiting staff members made note that the kiosk interface was intuitive, and that the kiosk would be a popular addition to the QEP foyer.

Enhance Overall Studio Experience.
Features like adding the facility map, and making a kiosk companion took into account the overall studio experience.

Inclusive Design.
Our visual design tried to consider accessibility. However, in reflection it felt like we only addressed surface level concerns. I would have liked to have done more research into different types of impairments, and how our design could be adjusted to be more inclusive.

The Run of Play Mobile

Building on research put together by a former colleague, I looked at technology’s role in youth sport leagues as the topic for an in-class interactivity project.

The aim was to research market opportunities, and address those opportunities with a mobile application.

Beginning with a straight forward question; how might we improve the youth sports team experience for the various stakeholders using available technology?

Audience & User Persona’s

Who's involved in youth sports? What kind of experiences do they have? What aspects do they enjoy, and which yield frustrations? I put together the above user persona cards based off of both research and interviews with people who are involved with various amateur sport teams.

People, Action, Context, Technology (PACT) Analysis

We would be designing for a wide range of individuals, of different ages, sizes, etc.

Assuming parents as primary users, their contexts-of-use would be busy, with a degree of stress having just finished work and needing to head to a game or practice. It would be rare if 100% of their attention were captured by the application during most use cases.

Their current use of technology is inconsistent and fragmented; at best parents may use a team centric app (like TeamSnap) for organization, and/or keep up with stats through a league-wide data system (for example, pointstreak.com is used in minor hockey leagues).

Defining the Problem

Research was focused around interviewing parents, players, coaches and fans, and trying to get a feel for the kinds of frustrations they experienced, and what they pointed to as pain points with the current technology that they used.

Two significant pain points stood out:

1. Constrained communication platforms
2. Lack of a feeling of Community Support
Sketching & Ideation

Designing for the two key pain points was paramount throughout the sketching and subsequent iteration phases.

Three flows were designed for the prototype:

1. Channel-Style Messaging
2. Public Team Profiles
3. Pre-Game Notification & Chat
High-Fidelity Prototype

Designing the application for an android system, it was practical to follow Google's material design guidelines.

For example, differentiating between when to use card elements vs. list elements was an important design consideration: cards were only to be used to imply that the element was dismissible.

Channel Style Messaging

Similar to the team communication app Slack, team parents and admin could open chat channels based on a topic, and set those channels to public or private. Public channels would appear on the team's public page, and supporters could also join in on the discussion.

Channel Style Messaging

Giving teams and players a public forum both allows for them to develop a better understanding of their league, as well as giving a platform for supporters to share in the teams progress and successes.

Pre-game Notification & Chat

The idea behind habit forming products is to align triggers with the frequency and occurrence of user habits1. A concept for doing so would be to automatically open game channels on the day of. The trigger would be a notification send out to parents/players declaring that the channel is open. Upon opening the chat, users will be greeted with a message posting relevant opposing team stats, among other information.

By regularly inviting users into an automatically created channel, the goal is for them to form a habit based inside the app.

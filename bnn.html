Business News Network Mobile

Introduction

BNN is Canada’s Business News Network, a news outlet/TV channel with a large following in the financial community.

As part of an in-class project, I chose the BNN mobile application as a candidate for a redesign. The focus of the redesign was to explore typography applied to smaller screens: what works, what doesn't, and why.

Critical Analysis

Examining the existing BNN application exposed numerous issues that could hamper usability. To name a few:

Typography

There are quite a few problems from a typographic perspective. On the home screen, article headlines are cut off.

The article body text has a very light color to it and tight leading ‐ even at larger sizes. These problems erode readability over the course of longer form articles.

Information Architecture

The home screen presents too many options to the user in a disorganized fashion. Markets, commodities, and Canadian Dollar Buys have their content hidden, and both the language and the design do not give clues as to what type of content is inside.

The navigation menu overlay also presents too many options, to the point of confusion. For example; at first glance, what is the difference between "Recommended" and "Top Picks"?¹. As well, some navigation categories are two-tier and three-tier, leading to a deluge of options.

Art Direction

Finally, the art direction is generic. This can be considered a missed opportunity to add delight to the user experience².

Aesthetic

The redesign aimed for cleaner, more organized screens — particularly the home screen.

The colour palette was brightened. The grey’s that were used in the original design were removed and the red used in the top bar navigation was swapped out in favor of white. I found that the white navigation bar was preferable for reading, as it did not call attention to itself, seemingly blending into the screen.

Custom illustrated icons and a careful selection of stock photography helps to add visual interest to the redesign.

Typography & Readability

The redesign incorporates Canadian typefaces: Laurentian STD, designed by Canadian Rod McDonald, was chosen as the body text typeface, while Toronto Subway was used for the Categories & navigation (Toronto Subway is a unique font designed for the Toronto Transit Commission in 1954³).

A type system was mapped, dictating the properties for each text category. Attention was paid to hierarchy, particularly on the home feed in order to direct the viewer’s eye to the most important content first.

Article readability was also a major consideration. First, the article reading experience was minimized, with the goal of allowing the text to fill the screen completely. Extraneous elements that could have distracted were removed.

Second, Laurentian was chosen as a highly legible serif font for long form reading. Taller x-heights and organic letterforms allow the font to be more readable at the smaller sizes needed for mobile. The text was leaded generously, while finding an appropriate font size required some trial and error.

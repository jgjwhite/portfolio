Business News Network Mobile

Introduction

BNN is Canada's Business News Network, a news outlet/TV channel with a large following in the financial community.

As part of an in-class project, I chose the BNN mobile application as a candidate for a redesign. The focus of the redesign was to explore typography applied to smaller screens: what works, what doesn't, and why.

Critical Analysis

Examining the existing BNN application exposed numerous issues that could hamper usability.

There are general problems with information architecture: the home screen hides content, and it is not immediately apparent that the market and TV schedule screens can be accessed by the three dot icon (top-right). These are important features that should be evident, to give the user an idea of the functionality available to them.

Navigation is cluttered and categories can be confusing (for example; what is the difference between recommendations and top picks?). Some navigation categories are two-tier and three-tier, leading to a deluge of options.

There are also quite a few problems from a typographic perspective. On the home screen, article headlines are cut off. The article body text has a very light color to it and tight leading ‐ even at larger sizes. These problems erode readability over the course of longer form articles.

Finally, the art direction is generic. This can be considered a missed opportunity to add delight to the user experience.

Aesthetic

The redesign aimed for cleaner and more organized screens, particularly the home screen.

The color palette is brighter; the grey's used in the original design were removed and the red used in the top bar navigation was swapped out in favor of white. The white bar is preferable when reading articles (does not call attention to itself).

Custom icons and a careful selection of stock photography (used as hero images for articles) aimed to add visual interest.

Typography & Readability

The redesign incorporates Canadian typefaces: Laurentian STD, (designed by Rod McDonald) was chosen as the body text typeface. Toronto Subway (a unique font designed for the Toronto Transit Commission in 1954) was used for the categories and navigation.

A type system was mapped, dictating the properties for each style in use. Attention was paid to hierarchy, particularly on the home feed in order to capture and direct the viewer's eye.

Article readability was also a major consideration. First, the article reading experience was minimized, with the goal of allowing the text to fill the screen completely. Extraneous elements that could have distracted were removed.

Second, Laurentian is a highly legible serif font designed for long form reading. Taller x-heights and organic letterforms allow for better readability at the smaller sizes needed for mobile. The text was leaded generously, while finding an appropriate font size required some trial and error.
